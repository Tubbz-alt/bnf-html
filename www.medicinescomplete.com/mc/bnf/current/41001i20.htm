Alcohol

Alcohol has the following interaction information:

ACE Inhibitorsenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with ACE inhibitors
Acitretinpresence of alcohol causes etretinate to be formed from acitretin (increased risk of teratogenecity in women of child-bearing potential)
Adrenergic Neurone Blockersenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with adrenergic neurone blockers
Alpha-blockersenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with alpha-blockers
Angiotensin-II Receptor Antagonistsenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with angiotensin-II receptor antagonists
Antidepressants, SSRIsedative effects possibly increased when alcohol given with SSRIs
Antidepressants, Tricyclicincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with tricyclics
Antidepressants, Tricyclic (related)increased sedative effect when alcohol given with tricyclic-related antidepressants
Antidiabetics alcohol enhances hypoglycaemic effect of antidiabetics
Antihistaminesincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with antihistamines (possibly less effect with non-sedating antihistamines)Sedative interactions apply to a lesser extent to the non-sedating antihistamines. Interactions do not generally apply to antihistamines used for topical action (including inhalation)
Antipsychoticsincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with antipsychotics Increased risk of toxicity with myelosuppressive drugs
Anxiolytics and Hypnoticsincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with anxiolytics and hypnotics
Baclofenincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with baclofen
Beta-blockersenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with beta-blockers Since systemic absorption may follow topical application of beta-blockers to the eye the possibility of interactions, in particular, with drugs such as verapamil should be borne in mind
Bromocriptine alcohol reduces tolerance to bromocriptine
Calcium-channel Blockersenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with calcium-channel blockers Dihydropyridine calcium-channel blockers include amlodipine, felodipine, isradipine, lacidipine, lercanidipine, nicardipine, nifedipine, and nimodipine
Carbamazepine alcohol possibly increases CNS side-effects of carbamazepine
Clonidineenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with clonidine
Coumarinsmajor changes in consumption of alcohol may affect anticoagulant control with coumarins Change in patient’s clinical condition, particularly associated with liver disease, intercurrent illness, or drug administration, necessitates more frequent testing. Major changes in diet (especially involving salads and vegetables) and in alcohol consumption may also affect anticoagulant control
Cycloserineincreased risk of convulsions when alcohol given with cycloserine
Diazoxideenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with diazoxide
Disulfiramdisulfiram reaction when alcohol given with disulfiram (see section 4.10.1)
Diureticsenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with diuretics
Griseofulvineffects of alcohol possibly enhanced by griseofulvin
Hydralazineenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with hydralazine
Hyoscineincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with hyoscine
Indoraminincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with indoramin
Levamisolepossibility of disulfiram-like reaction when alcohol given with levamisole
Lofexidineincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with lofexidine
MAOIssome beverages containing alcohol and some dealcoholised beverages contain tyramine which interacts with MAOIs (hypertensive crisis)—if no tyramine, enhanced hypotensive effectFor interactions of reversible MAO-A inhibitors (RIMAs) see Moclobemide, and for interactions of MAO-B inhibitors see Rasagiline and Selegiline; the antibacterial Linezolid is a reversible, non-selective MAO inhibitor
Metforminincreased risk of lactic acidosis when alcohol given with metformin
Methocarbamolincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with methocarbamol
Methyldopaenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with methyldopa
Methylphenidate alcohol possibly enhances effects of methylphenidate
Metoclopramideabsorption of alcohol possibly increased by metoclopramide
Metronidazoledisulfiram-like reaction when alcohol given with metronidazole Interactions do not apply to topical metronidazole preparations
Minoxidilenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with minoxidil
Mirtazapineincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with mirtazapine
Moxonidineenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with moxonidine
Nicorandil alcohol possibly enhances hypotensive effect of nicorandil
Nitratesenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with nitrates
Opioid Analgesicsenhanced hypotensive and sedative effects when alcohol given with opioid analgesics
Paraldehydeincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with paraldehyde
Phenindionemajor changes in consumption of alcohol may affect anticoagulant control with phenindione Change in patient’s clinical condition particularly associated with liver disease, intercurrent illness, or drug administration, necessitates more frequent testing. Major changes in diet (especially involving salads and vegetables) and in alcohol consumption may also affect anticoagulant control
Phenobarbitalincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with phenobarbital
Phenytoinchronic heavy consumption of alcohol possibly reduces plasma concentration of phenytoin
Procarbazinedisulfiram-like reaction when alcohol given with procarbazine
Retigabineincreased risk of blurred vision when alcohol given with retigabine
Sodium Nitroprussideenhanced hypotensive effect when alcohol given with sodium nitroprusside
Tinidazolepossibility of disulfiram-like reaction when alcohol given with tinidazole
Tizanidineincreased sedative effect when alcohol given with tizanidine
Verapamilplasma concentration of alcohol possibly increased by verapamil
