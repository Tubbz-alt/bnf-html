Opioid Analgesics

Opioid Analgesics has the following interaction information:

Alcoholenhanced hypotensive and sedative effects when opioid analgesics given with alcohol
Anaesthetics, General (intravenous) opioid analgesics possibly enhance effects of intravenous general anaesthetics
Anaesthetics, General (volatile liquids) opioid analgesics possibly enhance effects of volatile liquid general anaesthetics
Antidepressants, Tricyclicsedative effects possibly increased when opioid analgesics given with tricyclics
Antihistamines, Sedatingsedative effects possibly increased when opioid analgesics given with sedating antihistamines
Antipsychoticsenhanced hypotensive and sedative effects when opioid analgesics given with antipsychotics Increased risk of toxicity with myelosuppressive drugs
Anxiolytics and Hypnoticsincreased sedative effect when opioid analgesics given with anxiolytics and hypnotics
Cimetidinemetabolism of opioid analgesics inhibited by cimetidine (increased plasma concentration)
Domperidone opioid analgesics antagonise effects of domperidone on gastro-intestinal activity
MAOIspossible CNS excitation or depression (hypertension or hypotension) when opioid analgesics given with MAOIs—some manufacturers advise avoid concomitant use and for 2 weeks after stopping MAOIsFor interactions of reversible MAO-A inhibitors (RIMAs) see Moclobemide, and for interactions of MAO-B inhibitors see Rasagiline and Selegiline; the antibacterial Linezolid is a reversible, non-selective MAO inhibitor
Metoclopramide opioid analgesics antagonise effects of metoclopramide on gastro-intestinal activity
Moclobemidepossible CNS excitation or depression (hypertension or hypotension) when opioid analgesics given with moclobemide—manufacturer of moclobemide advises consider reducing dose of opioid analgesics
Selegilineavoidance of opioid analgesics advised by manufacturer of selegiline Selegiline is a MAO-B inhibitor
Sodium Oxybate opioid analgesics enhance effects of sodium oxybate (avoid concomitant use)

Opioid Analgesics belongs to Analgesics but Analgesics has no interactions information.

Tapentadol belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Alfentanil belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Diltiazemmetabolism of alfentanil inhibited by diltiazem (risk of prolonged or delayed respiratory depression)
Erythromycinplasma concentration of alfentanil increased by erythromycin Interactions do not apply to small amounts of erythromycin used topically
Fluconazolemetabolism of alfentanil inhibited by fluconazole (risk of prolonged or delayed respiratory depression)In general, fluconazole interactions relate to multiple-dose treatment
Itraconazolemetabolism of alfentanil possibly inhibited by itraconazole
Rifampicinmetabolism of alfentanil accelerated by rifampicin (reduced effect)
Ritonavirplasma concentration of alfentanil increased by ritonavir
Saquinavirincreased risk of ventricular arrhythmias when alfentanil given with saquinavir—avoid concomitant use
Voriconazoleplasma concentration of alfentanil increased by voriconazole (consider reducing dose of alfentanil)

Buprenorphine belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Ketoconazolemetabolism of buprenorphine inhibited by ketoconazole (reduce dose of buprenorphine)
Ritonavirplasma concentration of buprenorphine possibly increased by ritonavir
Tipranavir buprenorphine possibly reduces plasma concentration of tipranavir

Codeine belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Antimuscarinicspossible increased risk of antimuscarinic side-effects when codeine given with antimuscarinics Many drugs have antimuscarinic effects; concomitant use of two or more such drugs can increase side-effects such as dry mouth, urine retention, and constipation; concomitant use can also lead to confusion in the elderly. Interactions do not generally apply to antimuscarinics used by inhalation
Rifampicinmetabolism of codeine accelerated by rifampicin (reduced effect)

Dextromethorphan belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Memantineincreased risk of CNS toxicity when dextromethorphan given with memantine (manufacturer of memantine advises avoid concomitant use)
Moclobemidepossible CNS excitation or depression (hypertension or hypotension) when dextromethorphan given with moclobemide—avoid concomitant use
Rasagilineavoid concomitant use of dextromethorphan with rasagiline Rasagiline is a MAO-B inhibitor

Dextropropoxyphene belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Carbamazepine dextropropoxyphene enhances effects of carbamazepine
Ritonavirplasma concentration of dextropropoxyphene increased by ritonavir (risk of toxicity)—avoid concomitant use

Diamorphine belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Dihydrocodeine belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Diphenoxylate belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Dipipanone belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Fentanyl belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Antifungals, Triazoleplasma concentration of fentanyl possibly increased by triazoles
Baclofenincreased sedative effect when fentanyl given with baclofen
Etomidate fentanyl inhibits metabolism of etomidate (consider reducing dose of etomidate)
Midazolam fentanyl possibly inhibits metabolism of midazolam
Rifampicinmetabolism of fentanyl accelerated by rifampicin (reduced effect)
Ritonavirplasma concentration of fentanyl increased by ritonavir
Saquinavirincreased risk of ventricular arrhythmias when fentanyl given with saquinavir—avoid concomitant use

Hydromorphone belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Meptazinol belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Methadone belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Abacavirplasma concentration of methadone possibly reduced by abacavir
Amisulprideincreased risk of ventricular arrhythmias when methadone given with amisulpride—avoid concomitant use
Antipsychoticsincreased risk of ventricular arrhythmias when methadone given with antipsychotics that prolong the QT intervalIncreased risk of toxicity with myelosuppressive drugs
Atomoxetineincreased risk of ventricular arrhythmias when methadone given with atomoxetine
Carbamazepineplasma concentration of methadone reduced by carbamazepine
Didanosine methadone possibly reduces plasma concentration of didanosine Antacids in tablet formulation may affect absorption of other drugs
Efavirenzplasma concentration of methadone reduced by efavirenz
Fluoxetineplasma concentration of methadone possibly increased by fluoxetine
Fluvoxamineplasma concentration of methadone possibly increased by fluvoxamine
Fosamprenavirplasma concentration of methadone reduced by fosamprenavir Fosamprenavir is a prodrug of amprenavir
Nelfinavirplasma concentration of methadone reduced by nelfinavir
Nevirapineplasma concentration of methadone possibly reduced by nevirapine
Paroxetineplasma concentration of methadone possibly increased by paroxetine
Phenobarbitalplasma concentration of methadone reduced by phenobarbital
Phenytoinmetabolism of methadone accelerated by phenytoin (reduced effect and risk of withdrawal effects)
Rifampicinmetabolism of methadone accelerated by rifampicin (reduced effect)
Ritonavirplasma concentration of methadone reduced by ritonavir
Saquinavirincreased risk of ventricular arrhythmias when methadone given with saquinavir—avoid concomitant use
Sertralineplasma concentration of methadone possibly increased by sertraline
St John's Wortplasma concentration of methadone possibly reduced by St John's wort
Telaprevircaution with methadone advised by manufacturer of telaprevir (risk of ventricular arrhythmias)
Voriconazoleplasma concentration of methadone increased by voriconazole (consider reducing dose of methadone)
Zidovudine methadone possibly increases plasma concentration of zidovudine Increased risk of toxicity with nephrotoxic and myelosuppressive drugs—for further details consult product literature

Morphine belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Baclofenincreased sedative effect when morphine given with baclofen
Esmolol morphine possibly increases plasma concentration of esmolol
Gabapentin morphine increases bioavailability of gabapentin
Rifampicinmetabolism of morphine accelerated by rifampicin (reduced effect)
Ritonavirplasma concentration of morphine possibly reduced by ritonavir

Oxycodone belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Rifampicinmetabolism of oxycodone possibly accelerated by rifampicin
Telithromycinmetabolism of oxycodone inhibited by telithromycin
Voriconazoleplasma concentration of oxycodone increased by voriconazole

Papaveretum belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Pentazocine belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Pethidine belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Duloxetinepossible increased serotonergic effects when pethidine given with duloxetine
MAOIsCNS excitation or depression (hypertension or hypotension) when pethidine given with MAOIs—avoid concomitant use and for 2 weeks after stopping MAOIsFor interactions of reversible MAO-A inhibitors (RIMAs) see Moclobemide, and for interactions of MAO-B inhibitors see Rasagiline and Selegiline; the antibacterial Linezolid is a reversible, non-selective MAO inhibitor
Moclobemidepossible CNS excitation or depression (hypertension or hypotension) when pethidine given with moclobemide—avoid concomitant use
Rasagilinerisk of CNS toxicity when pethidine given with rasagiline (avoid pethidine for 2 weeks after rasagiline)Rasagiline is a MAO-B inhibitor
Ritonavirplasma concentration of pethidine reduced by ritonavir , but plasma concentration of toxic pethidine metabolite increased (avoid concomitant use)
Selegilinehyperpyrexia and CNS toxicity reported when pethidine given with selegiline (avoid concomitant use)Selegiline is a MAO-B inhibitor

Phenazocine belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Remifentanil belongs to Opioid Analgesics but has no specific interaction information.

Tramadol belongs to Opioid Analgesics and has the following interaction information:

Antidepressants, SSRIincreased risk of CNS toxicity when tramadol given with SSRIs
Antidepressants, Tricyclicincreased risk of CNS toxicity when tramadol given with tricyclics
Antipsychoticsincreased risk of convulsions when tramadol given with antipsychotics Increased risk of toxicity with myelosuppressive drugs
Atomoxetinepossible increased risk of convulsions when tramadol given with atomoxetine
Carbamazepineeffects of tramadol reduced by carbamazepine
Coumarins tramadol enhances anticoagulant effect of coumarins Change in patient’s clinical condition, particularly associated with liver disease, intercurrent illness, or drug administration, necessitates more frequent testing. Major changes in diet (especially involving salads and vegetables) and in alcohol consumption may also affect anticoagulant control
Duloxetinepossible increased serotonergic effects when tramadol given with duloxetine
MAOIspossible increased serotonergic effects and increased risk of convulsions when tramadol given with MAOIs—some manufacturers advise avoid concomitant use and for 2 weeks after stopping MAOIsFor interactions of reversible MAO-A inhibitors (RIMAs) see Moclobemide, and for interactions of MAO-B inhibitors see Rasagiline and Selegiline; the antibacterial Linezolid is a reversible, non-selective MAO inhibitor
Mirtazapinepossible increased serotonergic effects when tramadol given with mirtazapine
Ondansetroneffects of tramadol possibly antagonised by ondansetron
Venlafaxinepossible increased serotonergic effects when tramadol given with venlafaxine
